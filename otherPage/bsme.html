Biomimetic Leading-Edge Tubercles in Fixed-Wing MAVs

Micro Aerial Vehicles (MAVs) operate at low Reynolds numbers, typically ranging from 104–105. At these conditions, aerodynamic flows are susceptible to phenomena such as laminar separation bubbles, which result in early stall and a deterioration in aerodynamic performance.

This study investigates the effect of humpback whale-inspired leading-edge tubercles on the aerodynamic performance of a fixed-wing MAV. The wing profile is derived from the S5010 airfoil and tested at a Reynolds number of 1×105. The wing features a rectangular planform with an aspect ratio of 1.5. Tubercles are applied with an amplitude of 0.5 cm and a wavelength of 4.29 cm.

Numerical simulations were conducted to compare two configurations: the baseline model (without tubercles) and the tubercle model (with leading-edge tubercles). Key aerodynamic parameters—lift coefficient (CL) and drag coefficient (CD)—were evaluated across different angles of attack.

Results showed that at a 25° angle of attack, the tubercle model exhibited a 3.13% higher lift coefficient than the baseline. Additionally, the drag coefficient remained lower for the tubercle model at key angles (15°, 20°, and 25°). Importantly, the stall was delayed by 3° in the tubercle model, highlighting its stall-resistant behavior.

These findings demonstrate that biomimetic adaptation through leading-edge tubercles can significantly improve the aerodynamic efficiency and stall characteristics of fixed-wing MAVs—making them better suited for applications requiring enhanced stability and endurance, such as environmental monitoring, surveillance, and disaster response.
