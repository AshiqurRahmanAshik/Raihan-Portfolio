Biomimetic Fixed-Wing Micro Aerial Vehicle (MAV)

The project's aim was to conduct analysis and design optimization for the aerodynamic performance of a biomimetic fixed-wing Micro Aerial Vehicle (MAV). This analysis was inspired by the leading-edge tubercles on the flippers of the humpback whale with the intent of improving the aerodynamic performance characteristics of small Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) through nature-inspired geometry.

This project addressed limitations such as poor stall performance, instability at low Reynolds numbers, and low maneuverability. By altering the leading edge of the wing with tubercle geometries, we anticipated improved stall behavior and lift-to-drag ratio, while achieving stable and efficient performance in flight.

My Role in the Project

I was responsible for the computational design and simulation phase of the project. I created multiple wing configurations with varying tubercle amplitudes and wavelengths using SolidWorks. These wing designs were then meshed and simulated in ANSYS Fluent using turbulence models like IDDES to capture unsteady flow separation and vortex dynamics.

Flow visualizations, pressure distribution analyses, and performance metrics such as lift coefficient (CL), drag coefficient (CD), and lift-to-drag ratio (CL/CD) were evaluated against a baseline wing. After multiple simulation iterations, we identified an optimal tubercle configuration that improved wing performance across a wide range of angles of attack, particularly in the post-stall region.

Experimental Validation

We also fabricated physical MAV wing models using lightweight materials and tested them in our university’s subsonic wind tunnel. The experimental outcomes closely matched our simulation results, confirming that biomimetic designs improved stall delay and lift generation.

These enhancements are especially valuable for MAVs operating in constrained environments like urban surveillance, disaster monitoring, and environmental data collection.

Key Takeaways

This project highlighted how biomimicry can significantly influence engineering design—particularly in fields like aerospace where performance and efficiency are critical. I gained valuable hands-on experience in simulation, aerodynamic analysis, and interdisciplinary problem-solving, which I am excited to further explore in future research and development projects within aerospace and UAV design.
